PPE Essentials

In machining, PPE is the last line of defense after hazards have been reduced by design and process. You still need it because chips, coolant, noise, and impact risks never go to zero.

Eye and face protection

Glasses vs. face shields

Safety glasses protect against impact and small chips. Face shields add splash and grinding protection but do not replace glasses — wear both for high‑risk operations like angle‑grinding or heavy chip streams.

  • Use clear lenses for indoor machining.
  • Anti‑fog helps around coolant.
  • Replace scratched lenses that distort vision.

Fit and care

Glasses must sit close to your face without gaps. Clean with mild soap and water; paper towels can scratch coatings. Store them in a case to prevent damage.

Hearing protection

Earmuffs vs. earplugs

Earmuffs are quick and consistent. Plugs seal best when rolled and inserted properly. Choose protection that delivers enough reduction for your machine’s noise levels.

Smart habits

Keep protection on during setup, dry runs, and cutting — noise exposure accumulates. If you need to hear instructions, use muffs with built‑in communication or step away to a quiet zone.

Hands and feet

Gloves

Use cut‑resistant gloves for handling raw stock and swarf. Do not wear gloves near rotating tools — they can grab and pull you into the spindle. Remove gloves before you approach a running machine.

Footwear

Closed‑toe or safety‑toe shoes with slip‑resistant soles protect against drops and slick floors. Avoid loose laces and frayed cuffs that could catch chips.

Respiratory and clothing

For mist or fine dust, follow your school’s guidance on respirators and ventilation. Tie back long hair, avoid dangling jewelry, and secure sleeves. Aprons help divert chips and coolant.
